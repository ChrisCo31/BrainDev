This concept of "shared world" is crucial, and it's what makes the Ontology different from a traditional data warehouse or semantic layer.  
  
For AI to be useful in enterprise settings, it needs more than data — it needs context. LLMs were not trained on your business's data or processes. The Ontology provides that context. When you build on the Ontology, you create a shared foundation that serves both humans and AI simultaneously.  
  
The Ontology serves different audiences simultaneously, and this is one of its most powerful properties:

- **For humans**, it translates complex multi-system data into familiar business terminology. A plant manager doesn't need to know which SAP table holds the inventory data — they see "Warehouse X" with "42 days of supply" and can act on that directly.
- **For AI**, it provides the business context needed to reason effectively. When an LLM has access to the Ontology, it doesn't just see raw numbers — it understands that a warehouse is running low, that it's connected to specific plants, that there's a lead time constraint, and that certain actions are available to resolve the issue.
- **For systems**, it provides a single source of truth that eliminates the fragmentation caused by dozens of disconnected enterprise applications.
- **For governance**, it provides clear visibility into what data exists, who has access to it, what actions are being taken, and by whom (or what).
