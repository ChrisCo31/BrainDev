Closing the loop from data to decisions to actions is hard enough with just people and software. But there's a reason this challenge is more urgent: AI has introduced a fundamentally new category of capability into the enterprise.  
  
Traditionally, there are two categories of effort in any organization: human effort and thought, and traditional computation — databases, rule engines, scheduled jobs. AI introduces a third: what we can think of as AI Labor. It's not simply faster computation, and it's not a replacement for human judgment. It's a distinct category that sits as a conceptual bridge between the two.  
  
What this means in practice is that building AI-powered operations requires what is essentially a rigorous form of alchemy: figuring out the right balance between human effort, AI labor, and traditional computation for each step of a workflow. And that balance isn't static — it shifts over time as you learn what works, as you equip agents with better tools and more context, and as you build confidence in the system's reliability.  
  
This is the design principle behind the platform: Foundry and AIP don't just give you a place to store data or run models. They give you an architecture where human effort, AI labor, and traditional computation can be composed, recomposed, and progressively automated — all on a shared foundation — Ontology. For the decision-centric systems.
